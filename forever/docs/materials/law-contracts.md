# Legal Reasoning with IRAC: The Case of the Broken Delivery Contract

## 1. One dispute, all of legal analysis

Karim's bakery contracted Delta Logistics to deliver flour every morning by 6 a.m. For three
weeks deliveries came at 9 a.m., and Karim lost morning sales. Can he cancel the contract and
claim damages? This course does not give you the answer to memorize — it teaches the METHOD
lawyers use to reach one: IRAC (Issue, Rule, Application, Conclusion), the framework taught in
every law school since Langdell's Harvard case method. Legal reasoning is a structured
argument, not an opinion.

## 2. Law is applied reasoning, not a list of rules

The common image — law as a big book of rules you look up — misses how law works. Rules are
general ("a party must perform its contractual obligations"); disputes are specific (a delivery
three hours late for three weeks). The lawyer's work is APPLYING the general rule to the
specific facts, element by element, and arguing what follows. Two competent lawyers apply the
same rule to the same facts and argue opposite conclusions — that is the discipline, not a
failure of it.

## 3. Issue: the precise legal question

IRAC begins by naming the ISSUE — the exact legal question the facts raise. Not "did Delta do
something wrong?" but "Does a repeated three-hour delay in a time-specified delivery contract
constitute a material breach entitling the other party to terminate?" A sharp issue statement
already contains the elements the analysis must address: time-specified, repeated, material,
termination. A vague issue produces a vague analysis.

## 4. Rule: the governing principle

The RULE states the law that governs the issue. Here: a party may terminate a contract for
MATERIAL breach — a breach going to the root of the contract — but not for a MINOR breach,
which entitles only to damages, not termination. Whether a breach is material depends on
factors: the extent the injured party is deprived of the expected benefit, whether damages can
compensate, and whether the breach was willful or repeated. The rule names the TEST; it does
not decide the case.

## 5. Application: map each rule element to a fact — where cases are won

APPLICATION is the heart of IRAC and where weak analysis collapses. Take each element of the
rule and map it to a specific fact:
- "Deprived of expected benefit": Karim contracted for 6 a.m. delivery specifically to bake for
  the morning rush; 9 a.m. delivery destroyed that benefit entirely, not partially.
- "Can damages compensate": lost morning sales are calculable, which weighs toward minor — but
  the benefit was the timing itself, which recurs daily and cannot be restored.
- "Willful or repeated": the delay happened for three weeks, not once — repetition weighs
  strongly toward material.
A conclusion without this element-by-element mapping is just an assertion. The application is
the argument.

## 6. Conclusion: what the analysis supports

The CONCLUSION states the outcome the application supports: because the delivery time was the
essence of the contract, the deprivation was total, and the breach was repeated over three
weeks, it likely constitutes a material breach entitling Karim to terminate and claim damages.
Note "likely" — legal conclusions are arguments about probable outcomes, not certainties, and
they follow FROM the application, never precede it.

## 7. The counterargument is mandatory

A one-sided IRAC is incomplete. Delta's lawyer applies the SAME rule to the same facts and
argues the other way: the contract did not explicitly state that time was "of the essence";
Karim accepted the late deliveries for three weeks without formal objection, arguably waiving
strict timing; damages CAN compensate the lost sales, making this a minor breach. A real legal
analysis argues both applications and weighs them — the skill is seeing the strongest version
of the opposing argument, not ignoring it.

## 8. Change one fact, change the outcome

Legal reasoning is tested by varying facts. What if the delay were 15 minutes, not 3 hours?
The deprivation element weakens — Karim could still bake. What if the contract said "time is of
the essence"? The materiality argument strengthens sharply. What if Karim had objected in
writing after day one? The waiver counterargument collapses. The same rule yields different
conclusions as facts change, and tracing which element each fact moves is how lawyers think.

## 9. The misconception that produces bad lawyering

"Knowing the law means memorizing the rules." Memorizing rules without the skill of application
produces someone who can recite the law and lose every case. The rules are freely available;
the value is in mapping them to messy facts, element by element, and arguing the application
against a competent opponent. A student who states the rule and jumps to a conclusion — skipping
application — has skipped the entire discipline. IRAC exists to force the step where reasoning
actually happens.

## 10. What the method leaves out

IRAC structures the analysis of a single issue against a stated rule, but real disputes tangle
multiple issues, competing rules, and facts that are themselves contested. IRAC is the honest
skeleton of legal reasoning — trusted for forcing element-by-element application and the
counterargument, and respected for leaving to judgment the harder questions of which rule
governs and whose account of the facts to believe.
