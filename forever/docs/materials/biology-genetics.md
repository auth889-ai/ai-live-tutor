# Genes, Inheritance, and Populations: The Rice Breeder's Problem

## 1. One field, all of genetics

Fatima breeds rice near Dhaka, selecting for flood tolerance. Every cross she plants is a
probability experiment; every harvest is a ratio she can count. Genetics is the one branch
of biology that is arithmetic all the way down — and every ratio here is countable, so
every claim is checkable.

## 2. Genes, alleles, genotype, phenotype

A gene is an instruction; an allele is one version of it. For flood tolerance, allele T
(tolerant) is dominant, allele t (susceptible) is recessive. A plant carries two alleles.
The GENOTYPE is the pair it holds (TT, Tt, or tt); the PHENOTYPE is what you see. Because
T is dominant, both TT and Tt LOOK tolerant; only tt shows susceptibility. Two plants that
look identical (TT and Tt) can breed very differently — the visible and the underlying are
not the same fact.

## 3. The monohybrid cross: the 3:1 ratio

Fatima crosses two Tt plants (both tolerant-looking carriers). The Punnett square pairs
each parent's alleles: T with T, T with t, t with T, t with t. Four equally likely
outcomes: TT, Tt, Tt, tt. Genotype ratio 1 TT : 2 Tt : 1 tt. Phenotype ratio: 3 tolerant
(TT, Tt, Tt) : 1 susceptible (tt) — the classic 3:1.

In numbers she can harvest: from 400 seedlings of this cross, expect about 300 tolerant
and 100 susceptible. Expect 1/4 × 400 = 100 pure-breeding TT, 2/4 × 400 = 200 carrier Tt,
and 100 tt. The square turns a cross into a prediction she plants and counts.

## 4. The test cross: exposing a hidden allele

A tolerant plant is either TT or Tt — identical to the eye. To find out, cross it with a
known tt. If the mystery plant is TT: all offspring are Tt, 100 percent tolerant. If it is
Tt: offspring are half Tt, half tt — 50 percent tolerant, 50 percent susceptible. So a
harvest of 200 test-cross seedlings that yields about 100 susceptible reveals a Tt parent;
zero susceptible reveals TT. The phenotype ratio reads the hidden genotype.

## 5. The dihybrid cross and the 9:3:3:1

Now two traits at once: flood tolerance (T/t) and grain size, big B dominant over small b.
Cross two TtBb plants. Each parent makes four gamete types (TB, Tb, tB, tb); the 4×4
square has 16 boxes. The phenotype ratio is 9 tolerant-big : 3 tolerant-small :
3 susceptible-big : 1 susceptible-small — the 9:3:3:1 that appears whenever two independent
traits assort. From 320 seedlings: 9/16 × 320 = 180 tolerant-big, 3/16 × 320 = 60
tolerant-small, 60 susceptible-big, and 1/16 × 320 = 20 susceptible-small.

Independent assortment is why: how T sorts says nothing about how B sorts, so the single
probabilities multiply — P(tolerant AND big) = 3/4 × 3/4 = 9/16.

## 6. Probability, not destiny

Ratios are expectations over many seeds, not guarantees for one. The chance the NEXT seed
from a Tt × Tt cross is susceptible is 1/4 every time, independent of the last seed —
genetics obeys the same multiplication rule as coins. P(two susceptible seeds in a row) =
1/4 × 1/4 = 1/16. A breeder plants hundreds precisely because any single seed is a gamble
and only the count is reliable.

## 7. Populations: allele frequencies

Fatima's whole field has 1000 plants: 360 TT, 480 Tt, 160 tt. Count alleles, not plants:
each plant has two. T alleles = 360×2 + 480×1 = 1200; t alleles = 160×2 + 480×1 = 800;
total 2000. Frequency of T = 1200 / 2000 = 0.6; frequency of t = 800 / 2000 = 0.4. The
two must sum to 1, and 0.6 + 0.4 = 1 — a built-in check on the count.

## 8. Hardy-Weinberg: the null model

If mating is random and nothing selects, the next generation's genotype frequencies are
p² : 2pq : q², with p = freq(T) = 0.6 and q = 0.4. Predicted: TT = 0.6² = 0.36,
Tt = 2 × 0.6 × 0.4 = 0.48, tt = 0.4² = 0.16. On 1000 plants that is 360 TT, 480 Tt,
160 tt — exactly the starting field. The population is AT equilibrium. When a real count
deviates from this prediction, THAT gap is the evidence that something — selection, drift,
migration — is acting. The null model earns its keep by being the thing evolution breaks.

## 9. The misconception that confuses every student

"Dominant means more common, or stronger, or better." None of those. Dominant describes
only which allele's phenotype shows in a heterozygote — nothing about frequency or fitness.
In Fatima's field the dominant T is common (0.6), but a recessive allele can dominate a
population: if susceptible t were at 0.9, tt plants would be the 0.81 majority while T
stayed recessive-masked. Frequency is set by selection and history, not by dominance.
Evidence beats the intuition: count the alleles, don't trust the word "dominant."

## 10. What the model leaves out

Simple Mendelian ratios assume one gene per trait, clean dominance, and independent
assortment. Real traits like yield are polygenic, linked, and environment-sensitive — the
3:1 is the honest skeleton, not the whole animal. Hardy-Weinberg assumes no selection in a
field whose entire purpose is selection. The models are trusted for the structure they
expose and respected for the messiness they postpone.
