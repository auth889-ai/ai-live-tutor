# Reading Like a Historian: The Partition of Bengal, 1905

## 1. One event, all of historical thinking

In 1905 the British administration partitioned Bengal. Was it an administrative convenience or
a deliberate divide-and-rule? This course does not hand you the answer — it teaches you to
reach one the way historians do, using Sam Wineburg's four moves (Stanford's Reading Like a
Historian): sourcing, contextualization, corroboration, and close reading. History is not a
list of dates; it is a method of reasoning from evidence.

## 2. History is an argument from evidence, not a story to memorize

The common image of history — a fixed story of what happened — is wrong about how history is
actually made. Historians work from PRIMARY SOURCES (documents from the time: letters,
speeches, records) and build arguments, which other historians contest with other sources. The
"facts" are the sources; the history is the argument. To learn history is to learn to weigh
sources, not to memorize someone's conclusion.

## 3. Sourcing: interrogate the author before the content

Before reading WHAT a document says, ask WHO wrote it, WHEN, and WHY. A viceroy's official
memo defending the partition and a Bengali newspaper editorial attacking it describe the same
event with opposite framings — because their authors had opposite stakes. Sourcing means
reading the byline before the body: the author's position shapes every sentence that follows,
and a claim's weight depends on who is making it and what they had to gain.

## 4. Contextualization: judge by their world, not ours

A 1905 document must be read against 1905's assumptions, not today's. The partition happened in
an era of imperial administration where "efficient governance" and "managing populations" were
openly-stated goals, and Indian nationalism was a rising force the British watched warily. A
phrase that reads as neutral bureaucracy to us may have carried clear political weight then.
Contextualization resists judging the past by the present — you situate the document in its own
moment's events and vocabulary before deciding what it meant.

## 5. Corroboration: no single source is enough

One source is a claim; agreement across independent sources is evidence. Did the partition aim
to split Hindus and Muslims? A single nationalist speech asserting so is not proof — but if a
private British official's letter (not meant for the public) also notes the political advantage
of the split, and the administrative boundary happens to track the religious demographic line,
the sources CORROBORATE. Historians trust a claim in proportion to how many independent sources
support it, weighing especially the ones with no motive to lie.

## 6. Close reading: word choice and silence

The exact words matter, and so does what is NOT said. An official document calling the partition
a response to "administrative difficulties" while never mentioning the province's political
activism is telling you something by its silence. Close reading attends to which words were
chosen over alternatives, what is emphasized, and what is conspicuously absent — the gaps in a
source are evidence too.

## 7. Putting the four moves together

Take the viceroy's memo. SOURCE it: written by the official who ordered the partition, for the
record, with every reason to justify it. CONTEXTUALIZE it: 1905, rising Bengali nationalism,
imperial anxiety. CORROBORATE it: against nationalist accounts and against the map. CLOSE READ
it: note it stresses administrative efficiency and omits the political benefit a private letter
admits. Now you can argue — from evidence, with the sources' reliability weighed — rather than
repeat a textbook verdict.

## 8. Perspectives are not equally reliable

Multiple perspectives matter, but "hearing both sides" does not mean weighting them equally. A
source with firsthand knowledge and no motive to distort outweighs a secondhand account written
to persuade. The nationalist and the viceroy both have stakes; a neutral census record or a
private letter never meant for publication may be more reliable than either. Weighing
perspectives by their reliability — not just counting them — is the historian's judgment.

## 9. The misconception that makes history feel useless

"History is just memorizing what happened, and the textbook tells you." This kills both the
interest and the skill. The textbook is one historian's argument from selected sources; it can
be — and routinely is — revised when new sources surface or old ones are re-read. Treating
history as fixed facts to memorize means never learning the actual skill: evaluating evidence
and constructing a defensible claim. That skill transfers to every domain where you must judge
a source's reliability — which today is all of them.

## 10. What the method leaves out

Sourcing, contextualization, corroboration, and close reading make historical claims
defensible, but the record itself is incomplete — the powerful leave more documents than the
powerless, and some voices left none. The four moves are the honest discipline for reasoning
from what survives; they are trusted for the rigor they impose on evidence, and respected for
admitting that the archive itself is biased toward those who could write and keep records.
