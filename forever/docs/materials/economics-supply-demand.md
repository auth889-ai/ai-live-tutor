# Supply, Demand, and Elasticity: The Dhaka Coffee Market

## 1. A real market to think with

Rina runs a chain of coffee stalls in Dhaka. Every business decision she makes — what to
charge, how much to brew, whether to fear a rival tea shop — is economics happening in
public. We will use her market for everything in this course, with real numbers throughout.

## 2. The demand schedule

A demand schedule lists how many cups per day customers buy at each price (price in BDT
per cup, quantity in cups per day):

| Price (BDT) | Quantity demanded (cups/day) |
|---|---|
| 100 | 2400 |
| 120 | 2000 |
| 140 | 1600 |
| 160 | 1200 |
| 180 | 800 |

The law of demand: as price rises, quantity demanded falls, holding everything else
constant. This table IS the demand curve — the curve is just these points drawn.

## 3. The supply schedule

Sellers respond in the opposite direction. The market supply schedule:

| Price (BDT) | Quantity supplied (cups/day) |
|---|---|
| 100 | 800 |
| 120 | 1200 |
| 140 | 1600 |
| 160 | 2000 |
| 180 | 2400 |

At higher prices, brewing more cups is worth the extra cost, so quantity supplied rises.

## 4. Equilibrium

The market clears where the two schedules meet: at a price of 140 BDT, quantity demanded
equals quantity supplied at 1600 cups per day. That pair — price 140, quantity 1600 — is
the equilibrium. At any price above 140 there is a surplus (sellers brew more than buyers
want); at any price below 140 there is a shortage (buyers want more than sellers brew).
For example, at a price of 120 buyers want 2000 cups but sellers offer only 1200, a
shortage of 800 cups per day.

## 5. Price elasticity of demand

Elasticity measures HOW responsive quantity is to price. The formula: percentage change in
quantity divided by percentage change in price.

Between 120 and 140 BDT: quantity falls from 2000 to 1600, a change of -400 on a base of
2000, which is -20 percent. Price rises from 120 to 140, a change of 20 on a base of 120,
which is 16.7 percent. Elasticity = -20 / 16.7 = about -1.2. Demand here is ELASTIC
(magnitude above 1): quantity moves proportionally more than price.

Between 100 and 120 BDT: quantity falls from 2400 to 2000, which is -16.7 percent, while
price rises 20 percent. Elasticity = about -0.83. Demand here is INELASTIC (magnitude
below 1): quantity moves proportionally less than price.

The same product can be elastic in one price range and inelastic in another — elasticity
is a property of a point on the curve, not of coffee itself.

## 6. Elasticity decides revenue

Revenue = price × quantity, and elasticity tells you which way revenue moves when price
changes.

- At 100 BDT: revenue = 100 × 2400 = 240000 BDT per day.
- At 120 BDT: revenue = 120 × 2000 = 240000 BDT per day (essentially unchanged — this
  range is close to unit elastic).
- At 140 BDT: revenue = 140 × 1600 = 224000 BDT per day.
- At 160 BDT: revenue = 160 × 1200 = 192000 BDT per day.

Raising price from 120 to 140 REDUCED daily revenue by 16000 BDT, because in that range
demand is elastic: the 20 percent price gain was beaten by the 20 percent quantity loss.
A seller who knows the elasticity can predict this before touching the price.

## 7. Movement along vs shift of the curve

The most common mistake in all of economics: confusing a movement ALONG the demand curve
(caused ONLY by the good's own price changing) with a SHIFT of the whole curve (caused by
anything else — income, tastes, prices of other goods, number of buyers).

When Rina raises her price from 120 to 140, buyers slide along the same demand curve from
2000 down to 1600 cups. Nothing about their income or preferences changed — only the price.

But when milk prices spike and every cup costs more to make, the SUPPLY curve itself
shifts left: at every price, sellers now offer 800 fewer cups. The new supply schedule
becomes: at 120 BDT only 400 cups, at 140 only 800 cups, at 160 only 1200 cups, at 180
only 1600 cups. The new equilibrium is where demand meets this shifted supply: price 160,
quantity 1200. The market price rose from 140 to 160 and daily quantity fell from 1600 to
1200 — not because demand changed, but because supply moved.

Always name the causal variable before saying a curve moved. If the cause is the good's
own price, it is a movement along; anything else shifts the curve.

## 8. Income and cross elasticity

When average incomes in the neighborhood rise by 10 percent, Rina notices quantity
demanded at every price rises by 8 percent. Income elasticity of demand = 8 / 10 = 0.8.
Because it is positive, coffee is a NORMAL good here (people buy more as they get richer).
At the 140 BDT price point, 1600 cups becomes 1728 cups after the income rise.

When the tea stall across the road raises tea prices by 10 percent, coffee demand rises by
5 percent. Cross-price elasticity = 5 / 10 = 0.5. Because it is positive, tea and coffee
are SUBSTITUTES: when one gets pricier, buyers switch to the other. A negative cross
elasticity would mean complements — like coffee and sugar.

## 9. Price controls

Suppose the city, worried about costs, imposes a price ceiling of 120 BDT per cup — it is
now illegal to charge more. At 120, buyers demand 2000 cups but sellers supply only 1200:
a persistent shortage of 800 cups per day. The queue outside Rina's stall is not a
mystery; it is arithmetic. The ceiling does not create cheap coffee for everyone — it
creates 1200 lucky buyers, 800 disappointed ones, and pressure for side payments or
black-market cups.

A binding price floor (a minimum above equilibrium) does the reverse: it creates a
surplus. Both controls trade the equilibrium quantity away for a price target.

## 10. What a model is

Every schedule above is a model: a deliberate simplification that holds other things
constant so one cause can be isolated. The model's answers are only as good as its
assumptions — demand schedules drift with seasons, advertising, and habit. The discipline
is to state the assumption, compute the consequence, and then ask which assumption would
have to break for the conclusion to fail. Models earn trust by being checked against
data, never by being drawn confidently.
