# Moles, Reactions, and Equilibrium: The Soap Factory Problem

## 1. One factory, all of chemistry's arithmetic

Karim runs a small soap factory outside Dhaka. Soap-making is chemistry with invoices:
every batch is a balanced equation, every purchase order is stoichiometry, every failed
batch is a limiting-reagent problem. Numbers first, names second — and every number in
this course survives an execution.

## 2. The mole: chemistry's counting unit

Atoms are too small to count one by one, so chemists count in moles: 1 mole =
6.022 × 10²³ particles. The bridge to the scale Karim can weigh is molar mass: sodium
hydroxide (NaOH, the soap base) has molar mass 40 grams per mole (Na 23 + O 16 + H 1).
So 80 grams of NaOH is 80 / 40 = 2 moles, and 10 moles of NaOH weighs 10 × 40 = 400
grams. Mass to moles and back — that one conversion carries the whole subject.

## 3. Balancing: atoms are never created

Karim neutralizes waste acid with base: NaOH + HCl -> NaCl + H₂O. Count each side:
1 Na, 1 O, 2 H, 1 Cl both sides — balanced as written, coefficients 1:1:1:1.

The combustion heating his vats: CH₄ + 2 O₂ -> CO₂ + 2 H₂O. Left: 1 C, 4 H, 4 O.
Right: 1 C (in CO₂), 4 H (in 2 H₂O), and 2 + 2 = 4 O. Balanced only with the 2s in
place. A balanced equation is a conservation law you can audit by counting — and the
count is checkable arithmetic, never opinion.

## 4. Stoichiometry: the recipe's exchange rate

The balanced equation IS an exchange rate between substances. From CH₄ + 2 O₂ -> CO₂ +
2 H₂O: every 1 mole of methane consumes 2 moles of oxygen and produces 2 moles of water.
Burn 3 moles of methane: it consumes 3 × 2 = 6 moles of oxygen and produces 3 moles of
CO₂ and 6 moles of water.

In grams: methane's molar mass is 16 (C 12 + 4 H). Oxygen gas O₂ is 32. So 48 grams of
methane is 48 / 16 = 3 moles, which needs 6 × 32 = 192 grams of oxygen. Mass in, moles
across, mass out — the same three-step walk every time.

## 5. The limiting reagent: why batches fail

Karim's assistant loads 4 moles of methane but only 6 moles of oxygen. The recipe demands
2 O₂ per CH₄, so 4 moles of methane WANTS 8 moles of oxygen — more than the 6 available.
Oxygen limits: only 6 / 2 = 3 moles of methane can burn; 1 mole of methane is left
unreacted, and the batch yields 3 moles of CO₂, not 4.

The intuition trap: "more total ingredients means more product." False — the SCARCE
ingredient relative to the recipe decides, exactly like 10 wheels and 3 car bodies make
3 cars, not 6. Always divide each amount by its coefficient and take the smallest:
methane 4/1 = 4, oxygen 6/2 = 3 — oxygen's 3 is smaller, oxygen limits.

## 6. Percent yield: the honest scoreboard

Theory said 3 moles of CO₂; the gas meter measured 2.4 moles captured. Percent yield =
2.4 / 3 = 80 percent. Real reactions leak: side reactions, incomplete mixing, gas
escaping. A factory lives on yield — at 80 percent yield, producing 240 moles of product
requires planning for 240 / 0.8 = 300 moles' worth of reagents, a 25 percent overbuy.

## 7. Concentration: molarity

Karim dissolves 2 moles of NaOH into 4 liters of water: concentration = 2 / 4 = 0.5
molar (moles per liter). To neutralize 0.2 moles of acid he needs 0.2 moles of NaOH,
which is 0.2 / 0.5 = 0.4 liters — 400 milliliters of his solution. Dilution is the same
arithmetic backwards: adding water raises volume, lowers molarity, moles unchanged.

## 8. Equilibrium: reactions that run both ways

Ester formation (a fragrance step) is reversible: reactants and products settle into a
balance described by the equilibrium constant K. With measured equilibrium
concentrations — products 0.6 M and 0.6 M, reactants 0.2 M and 0.3 M — 
K = (0.6 × 0.6) / (0.2 × 0.3) = 0.36 / 0.06 = 6. K above 1 means products are favored
at equilibrium.

Le Chatelier's principle is the factory lever: add more reactant and the balance shifts
toward products; remove product as it forms and the reaction keeps producing to replace
it. Karim distills the ester out continuously precisely to keep the system chasing a
balance it never reaches.

## 9. The misconception that survives every classroom

"Equilibrium means the reaction stopped." No — at equilibrium BOTH directions keep
running at equal rates; the concentrations hold still because the flows cancel, like a
sink with the tap matching the drain. The evidence: drop in a tracer molecule at
equilibrium and it distributes into products anyway — motion without net change.

## 10. What the model leaves out

Stoichiometry assumes complete mixing, pure reagents, and reactions that go where the
equation points. Real vats have hot spots, impurities, and kinetics — the equation says
what CAN happen and in what ratio, never how fast. Rate is a different course; ratio is
this one, and the ratios are checkable to the last gram.
